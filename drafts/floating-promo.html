How CISOs Build Lasting Resilience in the AI Era

By Michael Fanning, CISO at Splunk

X Profile LinkedIn Profile

The CISO Report 2026

Learn how CISOs are transforming AI risk into AI-powered resilience.

Read the report

For CISOs, the only constant is continual transformation. Although our mandate is risk mitigation, it was often reactive in nature. In 2026, the CISO's function as a strategic architect and business enabler is intensifying, with new requirements to secure AI initiatives and further drive digital resilience. The role has transcended mere cyber defense.

This evolution signals deeper organizational duties, involvement, and impact. For instance, a staggering 96% of CISOs are now responsible for AI governance and risk management, which requires us to shape policy, vet models, and ensure secure adoption. This shift is happening against a backdrop of escalating challenges like increasingly sophisticated threat actor capabilities, quantifying and communicating ROI to leadership, and addressing the cybersecurity burnout epidemic. It's an uphill climb, but CISOs are forging on.

To understand how they're navigating this expanded scope, a survey of 650 global CISOs was conducted for The CISO Report: From Risk to Resilience in the AI Era. What we found was that the most impactful security leaders aren't just enduring; they're innovating — leading their organizations into a future defined by intelligent, secure operations.

The intersection of AI and cybersecurity is creating both unprecedented risks and remarkable opportunities. Security leaders who can harness AI's power while defending against AI-enhanced threats will define the next generation of enterprise security. This report explores how leading CISOs are building adaptive, resilient security programs that can withstand the pressures of 2026 and beyond.

AI-native threats are rewriting the rules of engagement. Adversaries now use generative models to craft phishing messages indistinguishable from legitimate communications, automate vulnerability research at scale, and even simulate trusted identities inside enterprise networks. The defensive surface has grown substantially, yet the time-to-detect window remains stubbornly wide at many organizations.

The CISOs who are winning this arms race share a common trait: they've embedded AI-powered detection into their core security stack and invested heavily in red-team automation. Rather than waiting for incidents to surface through alert queues, their platforms proactively hunt for anomalies and correlate weak signals across millions of events per second.

Talent remains the most constrained resource in the field. Sixty-three percent of respondents cited difficulty hiring qualified analysts as their top operational challenge, ahead of budget constraints and legacy infrastructure debt. In response, leading programs are deploying AI co-pilots for tier-1 analysts, reducing the mean time to triage by 40% and letting human talent focus on complex investigations that require contextual judgment.

Board-level accountability is another defining shift of the current era. In 58% of organizations surveyed, the CISO now presents quarterly to the board, up from 31% just three years ago. This increased visibility is a double-edged sword — it creates leverage to drive organizational change, but it also raises expectations for measurable outcomes that go beyond incident counts and patch velocity.
